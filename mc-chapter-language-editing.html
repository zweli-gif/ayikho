Part 1 — Grammar
The Rules That Matter

In business writing, grammar errors don't just lose marks — they make you and your company look unprofessional. One HR manager at Capitec said she bins CVs with grammar mistakes before even reading the experience section.

⚖️
Subject-Verb Agreement
The subject and verb must MATCH. Singular subject = singular verb. Plural subject = plural verb.
❌ "The manager are in a meeting."
(manager = singular, but 'are' = plural)
✅ "The manager is in a meeting."
⏰
Tense Consistency
Don't switch tenses in the same sentence or paragraph. Pick past OR present — and stay there.
❌ "She wrote the report and sends it to the manager."
✅ "She wrote the report and sent it to the manager."
👉
Pronoun Reference
It must be crystal clear who "he", "she", "it", "they" refers to.
❌ "Naledi and Zandile discussed the project. She said it would take two weeks." (Which she??)
✅ "Naledi said the project would take two weeks."

🔍 Proofreading in Action
Naledi Edits a Letter to the CEO
Naledi is a PA at Standard Bank. Her manager asks her to proofread a letter before it goes to the CEO. Here's her process:
1
First Pass — Grammar
She checks every sentence for subject-verb agreement and tense consistency.
She finds: "The departments has submitted"
Fixed: "The departments have submitted" (plural = have)
2
Second Pass — Punctuation
Checking colons, commas, apostrophes.
She finds: "its a pleasure to confirm"
Fixed: "It's a pleasure to confirm" (it's = it is, needs apostrophe)
3
Third Pass — Spelling
Reading slowly, word by word.
She finds: "recieve"
Fixed: "receive" (i before e except after c)
4
Final Check — Consistency
Does the tone stay formal throughout? Are job titles capitalised consistently? Does the date format match SA standard?
Good proofreading takes time — but one professional letter can open a career door.

Part 2 — Punctuation & Editing
Punctuation That Trips People
• Full Stop (.)
Ends a statement. Every sentence ends with one. "The meeting is at 14:00."
, Comma (,)
Short pause. Separates items in a list, and joins clauses. "Please bring your ID, payslip, and proof of address."
: Colon (:)
Introduces a list or explanation. After salutation in SA letters. "Dear Ms Khumalo:"
' Apostrophe (')
Two uses: Contraction (it's = it is) and Possession (the manager's office = office belonging to the manager).
Common mistake: "its" (belonging to it) has NO apostrophe.
Editing vs Proofreading
EditingProofreading
Big picture — ideas, structure, clarityDetail — grammar, spelling, punctuation
"Does this make sense?""Is every word correct?"
Done firstDone last

1 of 3
Which sentence is grammatically CORRECT?
A
The manager and her assistant was late for the meeting.
B
The managers is discussing the budget.
C
The team has submitted their reports on time.
D
Each of the employees are responsible for their own keys.
💡 Explanation
'The team has submitted their reports' — 'team' is treated as a singular collective noun, so 'has' is correct. Option A: 'manager and assistant' = plural → needs 'were'. Option B: 'managers' = plural → needs 'are'. Option D: 'each' = singular → needs 'is'.

2 of 3
Which sentence has a TENSE ERROR?
A
She submitted the report and left the office.
B
He wrote the letter and sends it to HR.
C
The manager will review the document tomorrow.
D
All staff must attend the training session.
💡 Explanation
'He wrote' (past) and 'sends' (present) = tense inconsistency. It should be: 'He wrote the letter and sent it to HR.' Always stay in the same tense within a sentence.

What is the DIFFERENCE between editing and proofreading?
A
They are the same thing — both check for errors
B
Editing checks grammar; proofreading checks spelling only
C
Editing checks clarity and structure; proofreading checks grammar, spelling, and punctuation
D
Proofreading comes before editing
💡 Explanation
Editing = big picture (does the document make sense? is the structure right?). Proofreading = fine detail (grammar, spelling, punctuation). You always edit FIRST, then proofread. Never the other way around.
