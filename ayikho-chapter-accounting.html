The Accounting Equation
1 of 2
The Big Idea
Everything a business owns came from somewhere
Think about it like this: if Sipho starts a car wash with R10,000 — where did that money come from? Either he put it in himself (that's Capital), or someone lent it to him (that's a Liability, like a bank loan). There's no third option.

That's the Accounting Equation. It says that everything a business owns (Assets) must equal everything it owes (Liabilities) plus what the owner put in (Capital). Always. No exceptions.
Assets = Liabilities + Capital
What you OWN What you OWE Owner's money
If this equation doesn't balance, something went wrong. An entry was missed, or a number was captured incorrectly. The equation is your built-in error detector.
The Three Parts
🏦
Assets
Everything the business OWNS or is owed. Things that have value.
Cash in the till · Stock on the shelves · Debtors (people who owe you) · Buildings · Equipment · Vehicles
💳
Liabilities
Everything the business OWES to outsiders. Money that must be paid back.
Capitec business loan · Creditors (suppliers you owe) · Bank overdraft · SARS VAT owed
👤
Capital (Owner's Equity)
The owner's share of the business. What's left if you paid off all debts.
Money the owner invested + Profits earned − Money the owner withdrew (Drawings)

Double Entry Principle
2 of 2
The Golden Rule
Every transaction touches exactly two accounts
When Sipho buys a pressure washer for R3,000 cash — two things happen at the same time. His cash goes down R3,000 (one account). His equipment goes up R3,000 (another account). One entry in, one entry out. This is double entry.

The reason this matters: if only one side gets recorded, the equation breaks. You'd have more equipment but your assets would look wrong. Double entry keeps everything honest.
Debit (Dr) — Left side
Credit (Cr) — Right side
Assets INCREASE
Liabilities DECREASE
Capital DECREASES
Expenses INCREASE
Assets DECREASE
Liabilities INCREASE
Capital INCREASES
Income INCREASES
Don't try to memorise the table above yet. Just remember the principle: every transaction has two sides. You'll get the debit/credit rules through practice.
What a T-Account looks like
Accountants draw accounts like a T — debit on the left, credit on the right. Here's the Bank Account for Sipho's car wash:
Bank Account
Debit (money IN)
CapitalR10,000
Loan — CapitecR5,000
Credit (money OUT)
Pressure washerR3,000
Rent depositR1,500
💡 Memory trick for the exam
"DEAD CLIC" — Debits increase: Expenses, Assets, Drawings. Credits increase: Liabilities, Income, Capital.
This comes up in almost every FA exam. Write it on your hand before you go in.
Exam callout
⭐ This is how it appears in FA N4 exams
You'll be given a list of transactions and asked to: (1) identify which accounts are affected, (2) state whether each is debited or credited, and (3) show the effect on the Accounting Equation. 10–15 marks in most papers.

Sipho takes a R8,000 loan from FNB for his car wash. What is the correct effect on the Accounting Equation?
A
Assets increase R8,000 · Capital increases R8,000
B
Assets increase R8,000 · Liabilities increase R8,000
C
Liabilities increase R8,000 · Capital decreases R8,000
D
Assets decrease R8,000 · Liabilities decrease R8,000
✅ Why B is correct
A loan means the bank gives Sipho cash (Asset goes up) and Sipho now owes the bank (Liability goes up). Capital doesn't change — the owner didn't put anything in. R8,000 = R8,000 + R0 still balances.

A business has Assets of R45,000 and Liabilities of R18,000. What is the Capital?
Use the Accounting Equation: Assets = Liabilities + Capital
A
R63,000
B
R18,000
C
R27,000
D
R45,000
✅ Why C is correct
Rearrange the equation: Capital = Assets − Liabilities. So Capital = R45,000 − R18,000 = R27,000. This is the owner's share — what would be left if all debts were paid.

Zinhle's business buys stock worth R2,000 cash. Which statement is TRUE about this transaction?
A
Total Assets increase by R2,000
B
Total Assets stay the same — cash decreases, stock increases
C
Liabilities increase by R2,000
D
Capital decreases by R2,000
✅ Why B is correct
Buying stock for cash swaps one asset (cash) for another (stock). Total assets don't change. Liabilities and Capital are untouched. The equation stays perfectly balanced — this is double entry in action.
