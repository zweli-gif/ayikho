Part 1 — Graph Types
The Right Graph for the Right Data

Choosing the wrong chart type confuses your reader. Each graph type has one job — know what it is.

📊
Bar Chart
Compares different categories side by side. Best when you have separate groups to compare. Can be horizontal or vertical.
✅ Comparing sales by department: Finance vs HR vs Operations vs Marketing
📈
Line Chart
Shows trends and changes OVER TIME. The line going up/down tells the story at a glance.
✅ Capitec's monthly customer growth from Jan–Dec 2025. You see exactly when growth spiked.
🥧
Pie Chart
Shows how a WHOLE is divided into parts (percentages). Every slice = a percentage. All slices must add up to 100%.
✅ Budget breakdown: 50% salaries, 30% rent, 15% stock, 5% utilities
📋
Table
Rows and columns for EXACT numbers. Best when precision matters and comparison is secondary.
✅ Employee records: Name | Department | Salary | Start Date
🧠 Quick Selector
Compare categories? → Bar chart
Trend over time? → Line chart
Parts of a whole (%)? → Pie chart
Exact numbers? → Table

🇿🇦 SA Worked Example
Capitec Savings App — Which Chart to Use?
The marketing team at Capitec needs to present data to management. For each dataset, we choose the right visual.
1
Monthly new accounts (Jan–Dec)
→ Line Chart
This is a trend over 12 months. The line shows whether Capitec is growing or declining month-by-month. Management sees the pattern instantly.
2
Market share vs competitors
→ Pie Chart
Capitec 38%, FNB 25%, Standard Bank 22%, Other 15%. Each bank is a slice. Total = 100%. Shows Capitec's dominance at a glance.
3
App downloads by province
→ Bar Chart
9 provinces = 9 bars. Easy to compare which provinces have highest adoption. Gauteng vs KZN vs Western Cape side by side.
4
Exact account balances per branch
→ Table
Branch name, number of accounts, total deposits — precise numbers that management needs for auditing. A chart would lose the exact figures.

Part 2 — Reading & Creating Visuals
How to Read Any Graph
1
Read the title first. "Monthly Revenue — Q1 2026" tells you what this is about immediately.
2
Check the axes (X and Y). X-axis = horizontal (usually time/categories). Y-axis = vertical (usually amounts/values). No labels = can't interpret the data.
3
Read the legend. What does each colour/bar/line represent? Critical for multi-category charts.
4
Identify the trend or key finding. Is it going up? Down? What stands out? That's the message the graph is communicating.
Creating a Good Visual
❌ Bad visual: No title. No axis labels. Unlabelled slices. Inconsistent colours. Hard to read font.
✅ Good visual: Clear title. Labelled axes. Legend. Consistent colour scheme. Data source cited. Clean, readable font.
📐 Rule: Every Visual Needs 3 Things
1. A clear title (What is this?)
2. Labelled axes or sections (What do the numbers mean?)
3. A legend (What do the colours/symbols represent?)

A marketing manager wants to show how Ayikho app downloads grew month by month throughout 2025. Which chart type is BEST?
A
Pie Chart — shows proportions of the total
B
Bar Chart — compares separate categories
C
Line Chart — shows trends over time
D
Table — shows exact numbers
💡 Explanation
A line chart is designed for showing change over time — perfect for monthly download data. The rising or falling line instantly tells the story of growth or decline. A bar chart would work but is less effective for time-based trends.

2 of 3
An HR manager has data showing that 60% of employees are permanent, 25% are contract, and 15% are part-time. Which visual is BEST?
A
Line Chart — shows the employment trend
B
Pie Chart — shows parts of a whole (percentages)
C
Table — gives exact salary figures
D
Bar Chart — compares departments
💡 Explanation
Pie charts are for parts of a whole — percentages that add up to 100%. Permanent 60% + Contract 25% + Part-time 15% = 100%. Perfect pie chart scenario. Line charts are for time trends. Tables are for exact numbers.

A student looks at a bar chart and can't understand what the bars represent because the labels are missing. What essential element is missing?
A
Title — the graph has no name
B
Axis labels — the X or Y axis is not labelled
C
Colour — the bars should be different colours
D
Source — no data source is cited
💡 Explanation
Axis labels tell you what the numbers represent — without them, you can't interpret the data at all. Is the Y-axis showing Rands? Percentage? Number of people? The labels tell you. This is one of the most common graph errors.
