Year-End Adjustments

Before you close the books at the end of the financial year, there's important work to do. You need to make adjusting entries for things like depreciation, prepaid expenses, accrued income and expenses, and bad debts.

TL;DR: Adjusting entries make sure your financial statements reflect what actually happened in your business during the year.
Why This Matters 💡

Example: Sipho runs a car wash. He bought equipment for R5,000 at the start of the year. By year-end, that equipment has worn down. If he doesn't record depreciation, his profit will be way too high, and his assets will be overstated. Not cool.

What You'll Learn

  • How to calculate depreciation using the straight-line method
  • Differences between prepaid and accrued items
  • How to journalise adjusting entries
  • Real-world applications from SA businesses

What is Depreciation?

When you buy an asset like equipment, furniture, or vehicles, they wear out over time. Depreciation is the systematic decrease in value of these assets.

In real life: Thabo's taxi repair shop bought a hydraulic lift for R8,000. After 5 years, it's still useful but definitely not worth R8,000 anymore. That's depreciation.

The Straight-Line Method

The most common way to calculate depreciation is straight-line. The idea: the asset loses the same amount of value each year.

Annual Depreciation = (Cost - Residual Value) / Useful Life OR Annual Depreciation = Cost / Useful Life (if no residual value)

Breaking It Down

Cost: What you paid for the asset

Residual Value (Scrap Value): What you expect to sell it for at the end of its life

Useful Life: How many years you expect to use it

Real Example: Sipho's Car Wash

Pressure Washer Equipment

Cost: R3,000

Residual Value: R300

Useful Life: 3 years

Calculation:

Annual Depreciation = (R3,000 - R300) / 3 = R900/year

Each year, the equipment loses R900 in value.

💡 Exam Tip: SARS doesn't always accept the residual value you estimate. Check current depreciation schedules. But for your N4 exam, stick with what the question tells you.

Adjusting Entries Beyond Depreciation

Depreciation isn't the only thing you adjust at year-end. There are four main types of adjusting entries:

1. Prepaid Expenses

Money you paid in advance for something you'll use over time.

Example: Zandile's Spaza Shop paid R1,800 for three months of rent in advance. But it's only month one. At year-end, only R600 has been used. The remaining R1,200 is still an asset (prepaid rent).

Adjustment: Debit Rent Expense R600, Credit Prepaid Rent R600

2. Accrued Expenses

Costs you've incurred but haven't paid yet.

Example: The electricity for December hasn't been billed yet, but you've used R400 worth. Your books don't show this yet.

Adjustment: Debit Electricity Expense R400, Credit Accrued Electricity R400

3. Accrued Income

Money you've earned but haven't received yet.

Example: You did consulting work for a client in December for R500, but they'll pay in January.

Adjustment: Debit Accrued Income R500, Credit Consulting Income R500

4. Provision for Bad Debts

Some customers might not pay. You estimate this and set aside a provision.

Example: You have R10,000 in credit sales. You estimate 5% might not be paid. Reserve R500 for this risk.

Adjustment: Debit Bad Debt Expense R500, Credit Provision for Bad Debts R500

Key Point: Prepaid = paid in advance (asset). Accrued = incurred but not paid (liability or income owed to you).
Worked Example
Zandile's Spaza Shop Year-End

Scenario: Zandile runs a spaza shop in Soweto. At 31 December, she needs to record adjusting entries for the following:

1. Depreciation on Equipment

Details:

Cost: R4,600 | Residual Value: R600 | Useful Life: 4 years

Calculation:

Annual Depreciation = (R4,600 - R600) / 4 = R4,000 / 4 = R1,000

Journal Entry:

Debit: Depreciation Expense R1,000 Credit: Accumulated Depreciation R1,000 (To record annual depreciation)

2. Prepaid Rent

Details: Paid R1,800 for three months rent in advance. Only one month has passed.

Calculation:

Rent Used = R1,800 ÷ 3 = R600

Prepaid Rent Remaining = R1,800 - R600 = R1,200

Journal Entry:

Debit: Rent Expense R600 Credit: Prepaid Rent R600 (To record one month's rent used)

3. Accrued Electricity

Details: Electricity used but not yet billed: R400

Journal Entry:

Debit: Electricity Expense R400 Credit: Accrued Electricity R400 (To record electricity used but not yet billed)
📚 Study Tip: Write out the full journal entry for each adjustment. Show the account names, amounts, and a brief description. This is exactly how SARS and your examiner want to see it.
