The General Journal
1 of 2
Ashki
Time to learn where we record transactions! 📒
⚠️ Pre-quiz reminder
The General Journal records non-routine transactions — focus on understanding this concept.
The Big Idea
The journal is your business diary
Think of the General Journal like a diary for your business. Every time money moves — someone pays you, you buy stock, you pay rent — you write it down here first. Date, what happened, which accounts are affected, how much. That's it.

The journal is the book of first entry. Nothing goes anywhere else until it's been recorded here. It's your proof that a transaction actually happened.
🇿🇦 In Real Life
Think of it like your bank statement on the Capitec app. Every transaction shows up in order — date, description, amount. The journal works the same way, except you also show which accounts are affected and whether they're debited or credited.
What a Journal Entry Looks Like
Every journal entry has five parts: the date, the account being debited, the account being credited, the amount, and a short description (called a narration). The debit always comes first.
🇿🇦 Real SA Example · Zandile's Spaza Shop
Zandile buys stock (bread, chips, cold drinks) from a supplier for R2,500 cash.
General Journal
GJ 1
DateAccountDrCr
Mar 1Trading Stock2,500
Bank2,500
Stock purchased for cash — bread, chips, cold drinks for resale
Why Trading Stock is debited: Stock is an asset. When an asset increases, you debit it. Why Bank is credited: Cash left the business. When an asset decreases, you credit it. Remember DEAD CLIC from Module 2.
💡 Quick Rule
Debit entry is always written first, on the left. Credit entry is indented underneath. Amounts must always be equal — debits = credits.
If your debits don't equal your credits, your entry is wrong. Full stop.

The General Ledger
2 of 2
⚠️ Pre-quiz reminder
A ledger groups transactions BY ACCOUNT — not the same as a journal which lists them by date.
From Journal to Ledger
The ledger sorts everything by account
So the journal records transactions in the order they happened — like a timeline. But what if you want to see everything that happened to one specific account? Like, all the money that went in and out of the bank account?

That's what the General Ledger does. You take each journal entry and "post" it to the correct account's T-account in the ledger. Same information, just reorganised.
🇿🇦 In Real Life
Think of the journal like your WhatsApp messages — everything in time order. The ledger is like your contacts list — everything sorted by person. Same chats, different view.
How posting works
When you "post" a journal entry to the ledger, you take each line and put it in the correct T-account. The debit entry goes on the left side of its account. The credit entry goes on the right side of its account. And you write where it came from as the "folio reference."

Notice the cross-referencing: In the Bank account, the debit side says "Sales" — because that's where the money came from. In the Sales account, the credit side says "Bank" — because that's where the money went. This is how you trace any entry back to its source.
⭐ Exam tip
In FA N4 exams, you'll get journal entries and be asked to "post to the ledger." They want you to draw T-accounts, put debits on the left and credits on the right, and include the contra account name. This section is typically worth 15–20 marks.
💡 The flow
Transaction happens → Record in Journal (book of first entry) → Post to Ledger (book of final entry) → Extract a Trial Balance
You'll learn Trial Balance in Module 4. For now, just lock in: Journal first, then Ledger.

When you "post" a journal entry to the ledger, what goes on the debit side of the account?
Think about the T-account structure.
A
The account that was debited in the journal entry
B
The account that was credited in the journal entry
C
Both accounts — debit and credit
D
Whichever account has the larger amount
✅ Why A is correct
When posting, the account that was debited in the journal goes on the debit (left) side of its T-account in the ledger. The credited account goes on the credit (right) side of its T-account. Each account gets its own T-account — debit stays debit, credit stays credit.
