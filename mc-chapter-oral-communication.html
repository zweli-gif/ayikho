Part 1 — Speaking
Your Voice is Your Brand

In the workplace, people form opinions in the first 7 seconds of a call or conversation. Formal, clear, confident — that's the standard.

📞 Telephone Technique

On a business call, you can't use body language. Your words and tone carry everything.

1
Greet professionally: "Good morning, Capitec Bank, Nomsa speaking. How may I assist?"
2
Listen without interrupting. Let the caller finish. Note key details (name, number, reason for call).
3
Speak clearly and slowly. No slang. No "ja sure", no "no worries". Keep it professional.
4
Summarise and confirm: "So I'll transfer your query to our loans department. Is there anything else?"
5
Close formally: "Thank you for calling Capitec. Have a great day." Let the caller hang up first.
🇿🇦 SA Contrast
Thabo's Woolworths Call
✅ "Good morning. I'm Thabo Mthembu calling about the supervisor role advertised on your website. I have 5 years of retail experience and I'd like to discuss the opportunity."
❌ "Hey um… I saw you're hiring? I dunno if you still are but like… I'd maybe be keen?"

The first Thabo gets the interview. The second Thabo gets a polite email saying "we've moved forward with other candidates."

🎤 Job Interview Prep
Zandile's Capitec Interview
Zandile (19, studying Management Communication N4) is applying for a Capitec customer service role. Here's how she prepares.
1
Research Before You Go
She Googles Capitec: their values, products, recent news. She can now say "I noticed Capitec recently expanded its Business Banking services — I'd love to be part of that growth." Research = immediate credibility.
2
Prepare Your "Tell Me About Yourself"
Structure: Past → Present → Future. "I completed my N3 at Ekurhuleni West TVET. I'm currently studying N4 Management Communication. I want to build a career in financial services because I'm passionate about accessible banking."
3
Body Language Checklist
✅ Firm handshake
✅ Eye contact (not staring — natural)
✅ Sit upright, slightly forward
❌ Don't cross arms (defensive)
❌ Don't look at your phone
4
End Strong
Always have a question ready: "What does success look like in the first 90 days for this role?"
Candidates who ask questions appear more interested and serious.

Part 2 — Listening
Active Listening

Most people listen to reply. Active listeners listen to understand. That's the difference — and the exam knows the difference too.

👁️ Give full attention
Put your phone down. Face the speaker. No multitasking. Show you're present — nod, make eye contact.
🔄 Reflect back
Paraphrase what you heard: "So what you're saying is the deadline moved to Friday — is that right?" This confirms understanding AND shows you were listening.
❓ Ask clarifying questions
If something is unclear, ask — don't guess. "Could you explain what you mean by 'urgent'?" It's professional, not rude.
🤐 Don't interrupt
Let the speaker finish. Even if you know the answer. Interrupting is disrespectful AND breaks communication flow.
🧠 Active Listening vs Passive Listening
Passive: You hear the words.
Active: You understand the meaning, emotion, and intent behind the words.
Examiners will give you a scenario and ask which one Sipho is doing. Always check: is he responding to the meaning, or just hearing noise?

A receptionist answers the phone at a law firm. Which response is most professional?
A
"Hello, who's this?"
B
"Ja?"
C
"Good morning, Lekgolo Attorneys, Siphiwe speaking. How may I assist you?"
D
"Hi, thanks for calling, what do you need?"
💡 Explanation
A professional business telephone greeting includes: time of day greeting, company name, your name, and an offer to help. Option C ticks all four. Options A and B are too informal. Option D is missing the company name and 'Hi' is casual.

Nomsa is in a meeting. While her manager speaks, she keeps thinking about her NSFAS application. What is she doing?
A
Active listening — she's in the meeting
B
Passive listening — she hears but isn't fully engaged
C
Encoding — turning the message into meaning
D
Decoding — interpreting the message
💡 Explanation
Passive listening is when you physically hear words but your mind is elsewhere — you're not fully processing the message. Nomsa is physically present but mentally distracted. Active listening requires full, intentional attention.

A
Checking your phone while someone talks to you
B
Planning your response while the speaker is still talking
C
Paraphrasing: "So what you're saying is the deadline is Friday — is that correct?"

Finishing the speaker's sentences for them
💡 Explanation
Paraphrasing confirms understanding and shows engagement — that's the core of active listening. Checking your phone, planning your response while they talk, and finishing sentences are all forms of NOT listening properly.
