What is a Trial Balance?
The concept explained
Ashki
Let's check if everything balances! ⚖️
A trial balance is literally what it sounds like — a balance check. It's a list of all the accounts in your ledger with their balances, arranged into two columns: Debit and Credit.
The golden rule of double-entry bookkeeping says: every debit must have an equal credit. So in a trial balance, the total debits should always equal the total credits.
Total Debits = Total Credits ✓
If they don't match? Boom — there's an error somewhere in your books. Not a big error, necessarily. But an error nonetheless.
💡 In real life
Imagine you're running a spaza shop in Soweto (like Zandile's). At the end of the month, you list every account — your bank, stock, rent owing, sales made, electricity paid. You add up the debit side, add up the credit side. If they match, you can sleep. If they don't? You've got homework.
It's called a trial because you're testing whether your books are correct. Not a perfect test — it won't catch everything — but a good starting point.

Why TB Matters
What it catches and what it doesn't
Here's the thing: a trial balance is not a complete audit. It won't catch every type of error. It's like a smoke detector — it catches some fires, not all.
✓ What TB catches
Posting errors (you posted a debit as a credit), calculation errors (you added wrong), incomplete entries (you recorded one side of the transaction but forgot the other).
Errors the TB DOESN'T catch:
Errors of omission: You didn't record a transaction at all. If you forgot to write up a sale, neither side is in the books.
Errors of commission: You recorded the right amount in the right accounts... but the wrong transaction entirely.
Errors of principle: You recorded a debit and credit in the right accounts, but those accounts are the wrong type.
Compensating errors: Two errors cancel each other out. R500 overstated here, R500 understated there. The TB balances, but you're still wrong.
This is why a balanced trial balance doesn't mean your books are perfect. It just means they're mechanically balanced. You still need to do more detailed checking.

⭐ This is how it appears in FA N4 exams
Trial Balance questions appear in almost every N4 paper, worth 10–20 marks. Common question types: prepare a trial balance from given ledger balances, identify errors when totals don't balance, correct errors and re-draft. Remember: the trial balance proves your double entry is correct, but it won't catch ALL errors (e.g., errors of omission or commission).

Question 2 of 3
Which of these errors would NOT be detected by a trial balance?
Correct!
An error of omission is when you don't record a transaction at all. Since neither side of the transaction appears in the books, the trial balance remains balanced. The trial balance only catches errors in transactions that are partially recorded.

Question 3 of 3
Where does the balance of the Sales account appear in the trial balance?
Correct!
Sales is an income account. Income accounts have credit balances in the trial balance. Expenses have debit balances. Assets have debit balances. Liabilities and equity have credit balances. It's all about the account type.
