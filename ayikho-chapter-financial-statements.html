Financial Statements

You've made it to the final module! Now you'll learn how to read and prepare the two most important statements every business owner needs. Financial statements are the final product of all your accounting work.

TL;DR

The Income Statement shows if you made profit or loss. It's a snapshot of your business's financial performance over a period (usually a month or year).

The Balance Sheet shows what you own, what you owe, and how much equity you have — at a specific date. It's like a financial photograph of your business.

Why Financial Statements Matter

Everything you've learned so far — journals, ledgers, trial balance, adjustments — it all leads here. Financial statements are how you tell the story of your business to the outside world. Banks, investors, tax authorities, and stakeholders all want to see these.

Think of accounting as building a house. Your journals are the raw materials, your ledger is the framework, your trial balance is the inspection checklist, and your financial statements are the finished house. The statements are what people actually live in and see.

The Two Main Financial Statements

The Income Statement (Profit & Loss Statement) answers one question: did the business make money this period or not? It shows all your revenue (money in) minus all your expenses (money out). The difference is profit or loss. This covers a period — a month, a quarter, or a year.

The Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position) answers another question: what is the business actually worth right now? It shows what you own (assets), what you owe (liabilities), and what's left for the owner (equity). This is a snapshot at one exact date — say, December 31st.

How They Connect

Here's the magic: the profit from your Income Statement flows into the Balance Sheet as part of owner's equity. So they're not separate documents — they're two perspectives on the same business. The Income Statement shows the journey (did we make money?), and the Balance Sheet shows where we are right now.

Real-World Example: Thandi's Hair Salon

Thandi runs a hair salon in Soweto. At the end of the year, she needs to prepare financial statements. Her Income Statement for the year shows: Revenue from haircuts and treatments was R95,000. She paid R30,000 for rent, R15,000 for products, R20,000 for electricity and water, and R5,000 for other expenses. Total expenses = R70,000. So her profit is R95,000 − R70,000 = R25,000.

That R25,000 profit gets added to her owner's equity on the Balance Sheet. Her Balance Sheet also shows her assets (salon equipment, cash in the bank, products in stock) and her liabilities (money she owes suppliers). The difference between her assets and liabilities equals her equity, which now includes the R25,000 profit she made this year.

Who Needs These Statements?

When Thandi goes to Capitec bank to apply for a business loan, they want to see her financial statements. They need to know: is the business profitable? Does it have assets? How much does it owe? SARS (for tax returns) also needs to see them. Even other businesses considering partnerships or suppliers deciding whether to give credit will look at these statements.

This is why accuracy matters. All those journal entries, all those adjustments — they all feed into these final statements. Get them wrong, and you're telling the world the wrong story about your business.

Income Statement

Part 1: The Profit Machine

Ashki
The big picture! Income Statement and Balance Sheet 📋

What is an Income Statement?

An Income Statement (also called a Profit & Loss Statement) shows whether your business made a profit or a loss over a period of time — like a month, quarter, or year.

Think of it like a report card for your money: it tells you how much you earned, how much you spent, and what's left.

The Basic Formula

Sales − Cost of Sales = Gross Profit
Gross Profit − Operating Expenses = Net Profit

Breaking It Down

Sales (Revenue): All the money you made from selling products or services.

Cost of Sales: The direct costs to produce what you sold (e.g., materials, labour to make products).

Gross Profit: Sales minus Cost of Sales. This shows profit before operating expenses.

Operating Expenses: Costs to run the business (rent, electricity, salaries, marketing, etc.).

Net Profit: Your final profit — what's left after all expenses. This flows into owner's equity!

💡 In Real Life: Zandile's spaza shop made R4,200 in sales last month. Her Cost of Sales was R2,100 (stock she bought to resell). Her Gross Profit was R2,100. Then she paid R1,400 in expenses (rent, electricity, delivery), leaving her with R700 net profit!

Balance Sheet

Part 2: The Financial Position

What is a Balance Sheet?

A Balance Sheet shows what your business owns and owes at a specific point in time — like December 31st. It's a financial photograph, not a movie.

It follows the fundamental accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity

The Three Sections

Assets: What your business owns (cash, inventory, equipment, vehicles, property)

Liabilities: What your business owes (loans, supplier invoices you haven't paid, rent owing)

Owner's Equity: What's left for the owner after all debts are paid. This includes opening equity + net profit from the Income Statement.

The Key Difference

Income Statement: A PERIOD of time (Jan–Dec 2024)

Balance Sheet: A POINT in time (As at 31 Dec 2024)

💡 In Real Life: On 31 Dec, Zandile's spaza shop had R15,000 in total assets (cash, stock, equipment). She owed R10,000 to her supplier. So her owner's equity was R5,000 (R15,000 − R10,000). That R700 profit from the Income Statement gets added to her equity!
Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity
R15,000 = R10,000 + R5,000 ✓ It balances!
