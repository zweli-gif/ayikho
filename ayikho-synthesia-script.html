Assets, Liabilities & Capital Explained
53s
1:12 — 1:25
Avatar holds up one finger — first item.
Let's break down the three parts. First: Assets. These are everything the business owns or is owed. For Sipho, that's his cash in his Capitec business account, his pressure washer, his buckets, his cleaning supplies. If someone owes him money — like a fleet company that hasn't paid yet — that's also an asset. We call those debtors.
On screen

1:25 — 1:38
Avatar holds up two fingers.
Second: Liabilities. Everything the business owes to outsiders. Sipho's Capitec loan? That's a liability. If he buys cleaning supplies from a supplier and they say "pay us end of month" — he owes them. That's a creditor, and that's a liability too. Even SARS VAT he hasn't paid yet — liability.
On screen
Gold card: "LIABILITIES — What you OWE" with icons: Bank Loan 🏦 · Creditors 💳 · SARS VAT 📄
1:38 — 1:52
Avatar holds up three fingers, then brings hand down naturally.
Third: Capital. Also called owner's equity. This is the owner's share. It's what would be left if Sipho sold everything and paid off all his debts. It starts with what he invested — his R10,000 savings — and it grows as the business makes profit, or shrinks if he takes money out. We call that drawings.
On screen
Green card: "CAPITAL — Owner's share" with formula: Investment + Profits − Drawings
1:52 — 2:05
Avatar brings all three together visually.
So to recap real quick — Assets are what you have, Liabilities are what you owe to others, and Capital is what's yours. Three buckets. Everything in a business fits into one of these. No exceptions.

Why Every Transaction Touches Two Accounts
55s

OK so here's where it gets interesting. Every time Sipho does anything with money in his business, two things happen at the same time. Not one — two. Always. This is called the double entry principle, and it's the reason that equation always balances.

Let me show you. Sipho takes R3,000 cash and buys a pressure washer. What happened? His cash went down by R3,000. But his equipment went up by R3,000. Two accounts changed. And notice — his total assets didn't even change. He still has R15,000 worth of stuff. Cash just turned into equipment.
