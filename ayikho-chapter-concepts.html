What are Accounting Concepts?

The rulebook for recording money

Ashki
Let's start with the building blocks of accounting! 🧱

Accounting concepts are the assumptions and principles that guide how businesses record and report their financial transactions. Think of them as the rules of the game.

The Entity Concept
Business money ≠ your money. Keep them separate.Your business finances and your personal finances are SEPARATE. If you own a spaza shop, the R50 you took from the till to buy airtime isn't business profit—it's a withdrawal. The shop and you are different entities.
In real life
When SARS audits Checkers' accounts, they don't care what Checkers' CEO spent on their holiday. They only look at business transactions. But if the CEO's holiday was a "business trip," then it's a business expense.
Going Concern
Assume the business will keep running — don’t panic-write-off assets.Unless you know your business is shutting down, you record transactions assuming it will keep running. You don't suddenly write off everything as "we're closing tomorrow."
In real life
Capitec doesn't record all its branch buildings as worthless just because there's a chance they might close a branch in 5 years. They assume the business is going to operate for many more years.

GAAP & Why It Matters

The global accounting rulebook

GAAP = Generally Accepted Accounting Principles. It's the international standard that says: "If you're recording accounting transactions, do it THIS way, not that way."

Historical Cost Concept
Record assets at purchase price, not current market value.Assets are recorded at what you paid for them, not what they're worth today. If you bought a taxi for R80,000 three years ago and it's now worth R40,000, you still record it at R80,000 (minus depreciation).
In real life
When a property owner in Soweto buys land for R500,000, that's what goes in the books—even if land prices skyrocket to R700,000 next year. You can't overstate assets.
Prudence / Conservatism
When unsure, understate profits and overstate losses.When in doubt, understate profits and overstate losses. Better to surprise people with good news than bad. Never overestimate what you're owed.
In real life
If a customer owes you R10,000 but might not pay (they're running away), you write off R10,000 as "bad debt." You don't hope they'll pay and record it as profit.

Real Life: The Township Tuckshop

How concepts apply to an actual South African business

Meet Zandile's Tuckshop (Alexandra Township). She sells snacks, airtime, and basic groceries. Let's see how accounting concepts apply:

Entity Concept in action

Zandile takes R500 from the till to buy groceries for her own home. This is NOT a business expense. It's her personal withdrawal. The tuckshop's books show this as a deduction from her equity (owner's drawings), not as a cost of goods sold.

Going Concern in action

Zandile's fridge is worth R8,000. Even though it's broken and needs repair, she records it at cost (R8,000), not as worthless. She assumes the tuckshop will be open next year, so the fridge will be fixed and useful.

Matching Principle in action

In December, Zandile sells R15,000 of airtime. The cost of that airtime (what she paid to Vodacom) is recorded in the same month, so profit = Revenue (R15,000) - Cost (R13,000) = R2,000 in December.

Prudence in action

Zandile gave airtime credit to her uncle for R800, but he's been unemployed for 6 months. She records R800 as a bad debt loss NOW, not "maybe we'll collect it later."

Quiz Question 1

Test your understanding

Ashki
Let's see what you know!
Zandile owns a spaza shop and a personal car. She uses the car to deliver groceries to customers 3 days a week. Where should the car go in the books?
A) In personal assets
Because Zandile owns it personally
B) In the shop's business assets
Because it's used for business purposes 3 days a week
C) Split between personal and business
3 days in business, 4 days personal
D) Nowhere—it's just a car
Accounting only tracks money, not assets
✓ Correct! The Entity Concept says the business and the owner are separate. If an asset is used for the business, it belongs in the business books. If Zandile had used the car purely for personal use, it would stay personal. But since it's used for business deliveries, the whole car is a business asset, and depreciation becomes a business cost.

Quiz Question 2

Test your understanding

A taxi operator bought a minibus for R120,000 in 2020. It's now worth R60,000 (market value). What's the book value?
A) R60,000
What it's worth today (current market value)
B) R120,000 minus depreciation
We use Historical Cost, adjusted for depreciation over time
C) Zero (it's a taxi, it breaks down)
Vehicles are too risky to value
D) R90,000
The average of historical cost and current value
✓ Correct! The Historical Cost Concept means we record assets at what we paid for them, then reduce that value by depreciation each year. So if the minibus cost R120,000 and has depreciated, its book value might be R90,000 (roughly)—not the current R60,000 market value. This prevents overstatement of asset values.

Quiz Question 3

Test your understanding

Capitec Bank estimates that R2.5 million of customer loans might not be repaid this year. How should they record this?
A) Wait until the loans actually default
Only record losses when they happen, not when they might happen
B) Record R2.5 million as a provision for bad debts NOW
The Prudence Concept: recognize losses as soon as they're predictable
C) Record R1.25 million (half the risk)
Spread the loss across the year
D) Ignore it, banks always recover some loans
Losses are part of normal banking
✓ Correct! The Prudence / Conservatism Concept means when a loss is likely, you record it immediately—don't wait for confirmation. Capitec records the R2.5 million provision now so their profit isn't overstated. This keeps the financial statements honest and protective.
