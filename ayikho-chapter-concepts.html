The rulebook for recording money Accounting concepts are the assumptions and principles that guide how businesses record and report their financial transactions. Think of them as the rules of the game. The Entity Concept Your business finances and your personal finances are SEPARATE. If you own a spaza shop, the R50 you took from the till to buy airtime isn't business profit—it's a withdrawal. The shop and you are different entities. IN REAL LIFE When SARS audits Checkers' accounts, they don't care what Checkers' CEO spent on their holiday. They only look at business transactions. But if the CEO's holiday was a "business trip," then it's a business expense. Going Concern Unless you know your business is shutting down, you record transactions assuming it will keep running. You don't suddenly write off everything as "we're closing tomorrow." IN REAL LIFE Capitec doesn't record all its branch buildings as worthless just because there's a chance they might close a branch in 5 years. They assume the business is going to operate for many more years. Got it, next → Match the Concepts Tap a concept, then its definition Test your knowledge. Match all 6 concepts to their meanings. 0 / 6 matched GAAP & Why It Matters The global accounting rulebook GAAP = Generally Accepted Accounting Principles. It's the international standard that says: "If you're recording accounting transactions, do it THIS way, not that way." Historical Cost Concept Assets are recorded at what you paid for them, not what they're worth today. If you bought a taxi for R80,000 three years ago and it's now worth R40,000, you still record it at R80,000 (minus depreciation). IN REAL LIFE When a property owner in Soweto buys land for R500,000, that's what goes in the books—even if land prices skyrocket to R700,000 next year. You can't overstate assets. Prudence / Conservatism When in doubt, understate profits and overstate losses. Better to surprise people with good news than bad. Never overestimate what you're owed. IN REAL LIFE If a customer owes you R10,000 but might not pay (they're running away), you write off R10,000 as "bad debt." You don't hope they'll pay and record it as profit. Next principles → Real Life: The Township Tuckshop How concepts apply to an actual South African business Meet Zandile's Tuckshop (Alexandra Township). She sells snacks, airtime, and basic groceries. Let's see how accounting concepts apply: Entity Concept in action Zandile takes R500 from the till to buy groceries for her own home. This is NOT a business expense. It's her personal withdrawal. The tuckshop's books show this as a deduction from her equity (owner's drawings), not as a cost of goods sold. Going Concern in action Zandile's fridge is worth R8,000. Even though it's broken and needs repair, she records it at cost (R8,000), not as worthless. She assumes the tuckshop will be open next year, so the fridge will be fixed and useful. Matching Principle in action In December, Zandile sells R15,000 of airtime. The cost of that airtime (what she paid to Vodacom) is recorded in the same month, so profit = Revenue (R15,000) - Cost (R13,000) = R2,000 in December. Prudence in action Zandile gave airtime credit to her uncle for R800, but he's been unemployed for 6 months. She records R800 as a bad debt loss NOW, not "maybe we'll collect it later."
