Departmental Accounts

Module 4 · Financial Accounting N4

🏪
Sipho's Hardware — 3 Departments
Tools · Paint · Electrical

What are Departmental Accounts?

When a business has more than one department (like a hardware store with Tools, Paint & Electrical sections), it keeps separate records for each department so the owner knows which departments are profitable and which are not.

💡 Real LifeShoprite tracks Bakery, Butchery, and Fresh Produce separately. If the Butchery is losing money, they know to cut it — without departmental accounts, they'd never know!

Why do it?

✅ See which department makes the most profit
✅ Control costs per department
✅ Identify underperforming departments
✅ Make better business decisions

⭐ Exam tipThe syllabus covers the periodic inventory system — this means stock is counted at the end of the period, not continuously tracked.

Columnar Journals

The secret weapon of departmental accounts

What makes them "columnar"?

Normal journals have one column for amounts. Columnar journals have extra columns — one for each department. Every transaction is split across departments in the same journal entry.

7 COLUMNAR JOURNALS
JournalRecords
📦 Purchases JournalGoods bought on credit per dept
↩️ Purchases ReturnsGoods returned to suppliers per dept
🛒 Sales JournalGoods sold on credit per dept
↪️ Sales ReturnsGoods returned by customers per dept
💵 Cash JournalsCash receipts & payments per dept
↔️ InterdepartmentalTransfers between departments
📝 General JournalAll other entries per dept
💡 Key PointEach journal still has a total column plus individual columns for Tools, Paint, Electrical (or however many departments there are). The totals must always equal the sum of the departmental columns.
⭐ Exam tipIn an exam question, you'll be given transactions and told which department they belong to. Just put the amount in the right department column — and always fill in the total column too!

The posting rule

At month end:
• Creditors total (R20 370) → posted to Creditors Control account
• Tools total (R8 400) → posted to Tools Purchases account
• Paint total (R3 150) → posted to Paint Purchases account
• Electrical total (R8 820) → posted to Electrical Purchases account

⭐ Exam tipEach department has its OWN purchases account and its OWN sales account in the ledger. This is what makes departmental accounts "departmental".

Interdepartmental Transfers

When stock moves between departments

What is an interdepartmental transfer?

Sometimes stock bought for one department is needed by another department. When this happens, you record a transfer — reducing one department's stock and increasing another's.

💡 ExamplePaint brushes ordered for the Paint Department are actually needed in the Tools Department. Sipho transfers R1 200 worth from Paint → Tools.

The journal entry

Interdepartmental Transfers Journal
Tools Dept (receiving) Dr 1 200
Paint Dept (giving) Cr 1 200
Narration: Transfer of brushes from Paint to Tools

The golden rule

The department receiving stock gets DEBITED.
The department giving stock gets CREDITED.

The total of the journal is always R0 because one department's gain is another's loss — no new value is created, it just moves.

⭐ Exam tipTransfers are done at cost price — not selling price. Never mark up when transferring between departments.

Departmental Ledger Accounts

What goes in each department's accounts

Each department has 2 key accounts

PURCHASES ACCOUNT (per dept)

Dr side: Purchases from Purchases Journal
Dr side: Transfers received from other depts
Cr side: Purchases Returns
Cr side: Transfers sent to other depts
Cr side: Closing balance → to Trading Account

SALES ACCOUNT (per dept)

Cr side: Sales from Sales Journal
Cr side: Cash Sales from Cash Receipts Journal
Dr side: Sales Returns
Dr side: Closing balance → to Trading Account

💡 RememberAt year-end, each department's Purchases and Sales accounts are closed off to their own Departmental Trading Account — not to one combined trading account.
⭐ Exam tipIf asked to post a transaction, ask yourself: which department? which side? Always label accounts as "Tools Purchases" or "Paint Sales" — not just "Purchases".

Departmental Trading Statement

Gross profit per department

What is it?

The Departmental Trading Statement shows gross profit for each department separately. It's basically an Income Statement — but only up to Gross Profit, and split by department.

Structure of Departmental Trading Statement
Sales
Less: Sales Returns
= Net Sales
Less: Cost of Sales:
  Opening Stock
  + Purchases
  + Transfers In
  − Purchases Returns
  − Transfers Out
  − Closing Stock
= GROSS PROFIT per dept

⭐ Exam tipGP% = (Gross Profit ÷ Net Sales) × 100. You will almost certainly be asked to calculate this in the exam.

Departmental P&L Statement

Net profit per department

From Gross Profit → Net Profit

The Departmental P&L takes the Gross Profit from the Trading Statement and deducts expenses. Some expenses belong to a specific department (direct expenses) — others must be allocated across departments.

DIRECT EXPENSES

Expenses you can trace directly to one department:
• Dept salaries, dept packaging, dept advertising

ALLOCATED EXPENSES

Shared expenses split using a fair basis:
• Rent → floor space (m²)
• Insurance → value of stock
• Electricity → usage or floor space
• Admin salaries → number of staff

Departmental P&L
Gross Profit per dept
Less: Direct Expenses
Less: Allocated Expenses
= NET PROFIT per dept
⭐ Exam tipAllocation questions are common. If Rent is R36 000 and Tools uses 60m², Paint 40m², Electrical 20m² (total 120m²) — Tools gets R18 000, Paint R12 000, Electrical R6 000.
